Over the next few days, the war situation in western Sicily took on an eerie calm.

As for Carthage, Mago was busy reorganizing the chaotic army after the landing and transporting the supplies scattered on the coast back to Lilibi. At the same time, he also took time to meet with officials from Carthage and envoys from the allies to learn more about Sicily. Due to the situation, he even summoned Arconis, the leader of Sikel who had been ignored by Monte Adeno.

On the Dionia side, Leotizides no longer sent troops to destroy his opponents' fields and villages, but ordered his soldiers to strengthen the city defenses.

In fact, he has been doing this since he captured Selinus, because he knew very well that the Battle of Minoa would not be enough to break Carthage's muscles, but would instead lead to a more violent attack from Carthage. Counterattack, the south coast of Sicily is the main offensive route of the Carthaginian army. Selinus, located at the starting point of this route, is very important. As long as it is held fast, the Carthaginian army can move further to Dio on the south coast. The attack on the territory of Nia's friendly city-states creates great obstacles, which helps to complete the strategic purpose given by King Davos.

At the same time, Leotizides also sent a messenger back to Catanai to inform Sipulus of the new war situation. He also sought reinforcements from friendly city-states such as Minoa, Agrigento, and Gela again to Strengthens Selinus's defenses.

Leoticides hopes that the battle will happen as late as possible, but Mago can't wait. After all, he made a promise in the Senate to drive the Dionians out of Sicily within one year. So three days later, the mighty Carthaginian army surrounded Selinus.

Mago ordered the army to start building the camp, and also asked engineers and carpenters to build siege engines.

After the camp began to take shape, he began to send thousands of light infantry formed by various Sicilian allies to gradually clear the traps and obstacles outside the city of Selinus.

Of course, Leotizides would not allow the enemy to approach the trench so easily. Therefore, he not only ordered the defenders at the top of the city to carry out fierce long-range attacks on any enemy who entered the attack range of the city, but also organized hundreds of troops from time to time. Thousands of light armored soldiers, taking advantage of the enemy's unpreparedness, went out of the city to attack the enemy who was busy clearing obstacles.

Leoticides' proactive defensive tactics inflicted considerable casualties on the Carthaginian soldiers who were clearing the obstacles below the city. However, over the next few days, Carthage's encirclement of Selinus was still gradually shrinking.

For Mago, who had an army of about 120,000, the few casualties were simply insignificant and did not have any impact on him. However, with the strong military power as a backing, the Carthaginians' cleanup work outside Selinus City was Has never been interrupted.

As the Carthaginian army advanced in an orderly manner under the city and strengthened its defenses against raids by the defenders, the Dionysian army also reduced the number of assaults out of the city after suffering one or two small losses.

Soon, the Carthaginians placed dozens of ballistas in front of the city and continuously bombarded the walls of Selinus with stone bullets.

Since the Dionian Army has city wall attack and defense training in its usual military training, of course they also have their own research on how to defend against city siege weapons-ballistas. In addition to having the ballistas deployed on the city walls and inside the city carry out precise strikes on the enemy's ballistae, Leotizides also asked the soldiers to collect a large number of fishing nets from the fishermen in the port, hang them from the city head, and cover the walls. to cushion the impact of stone bullets.
